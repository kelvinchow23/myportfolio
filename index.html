Reconfigurable Display

Can you fit a laptop into your pocket?

Moore's law drives technological advancement to make electronics smaller, true for most cases. Yet, the cellphone, arguably the most pervasive electronic device, defies this trend. Why is this? One reason is the trade-off between convenience and practicality. People would rather carry around a lightweight phone in their pocket. Most people also want large electronic displays, which offer more utility. The electronic display is the geometric constraint of cell phones, tablets, and laptops. A device with a reconfigurable display would maximize the convenience and utility benefit of electronic devices.

The general approach in this field is to place rigid LEDs on soft flexible, stretchable substrates. The key issue with this approach is once the substrate is stretched, the pixel density decreases, reducing the image quality. Also, this approach is dependent on the substrate's mechanical properties, which diminish when stretched. This project proposes a different approach to this problem.

The Initial Design

The first design proposed a solution where 16 individual square "islands", each representing an LED, would be constrained to move uniformly. Each island was constrained to move linearly on a track and connected to other islands via guide arms. By moving one island, all islands would move simultaneously.

Two major problems of this design forced a redesign. First, this design required extra space in order to work, defeating the purpose of having a compact design. Second, this design shares the same flaw as the flexible substrate design, where there are gaps in between islands, reducing pixel density when stretched.

Re-Designing the Prototype

The complete assembly of the prototype is shown on the left. From left to right, the figure shows a transition from collapsed to expanded state. Depending on which direction the force is applied, the display prototype can either be stretched from its collapsed 3x3 state to either a 5x5 or a 3x5 shape (shown in a later slide). Different aspect ratios are beneficial for different applications.

This limitation of this design is the large height. The height to pixel area needs to be reduced, or else these displays would be cube-like objects. Nevertheless, this design shows a different, counterintuitive approach to flexible displays.

Balance Rehabilitation Game

Individuals suffering from spinal cord injuries (SCI) often have balance instability caused by a reduced neural control of lower-limbed muscles. Rehabilitation tools have been demonstrated to be effective in repairing the disconnect between brain and body. Two rehabilitation tools of interest are visual feedback training (VFT) and functional electrical stimulation (FES) therapy. A commercial example of VFT is Wii Fit, where some of the games can be used to improve balance. A commercial example of FES is the MyndMove, where electrodes are placed on the body to activate certain muscle groups, producing functional movements.

VFT + FES Therapy

We propose a balance rehabilitation tool that combines both visual feedback training with functional electrical stimulation therapy. Our hypothesis is By combining an engaging training game with stimulation therapy, we hypothesize our balance training tool would amplify and accelerate the rehabilitation effects compared to single-modal approaches. For more details, refer here.

Four Task-Oriented Games

One training session includes 4 games played 3 times each. The games are designed to emulate a collection of muscle strengthening exercises for balance rehabilitation. Each game displays a transient coloured dot on the screen, displaying the individual's centre of position on the force plate.

TARGET: This game trains an individual to stand as still as possible in the optimal balance position. The objective is to keep the red dot as close to the centre as possible.

HUNTING: A randomly spawning blue target appears and the objective is to move the red dot inside that target and hold it for 3 seconds. Once successful, a new target appears in a different location. This game trains muscle reflexes by inducing constant shifting of body weight.

ELLIPSE: A target follows an elliptical orbit at the edge of an individual's range of motion. While the dot is in the target, the target position increments along the orbit path. This forces the participant to keep their centre of balance away from the middle for the entire duration of the game.

COLOUR MATCHING: In this game, the dot changes colour. The individual needs to move the dot in the matching coloured target. Once successful, the dot changes to a different colour. This game requires some mental processing as well as causing drastic shifts in body weight.

Two Balance Assessments

In addition to the games, a training session includes two balance assessments to track progress of balance improvement over multiple training sessions. These assessments are standard tests that have a strong correlation to an individual's balance ability.

Static Balance Assessment

An individual is asked to stand on the force plate with their eyes closed as still as possible for 100 seconds. During this time, a centre of position (COP) time series will be recorded to track the involuntary sway of the individual. For these studies, three performance indicators are taken: Root means squared in the (1) Anterior-posterior (front to back) and (2) Medial-Lateral (side to side) direction, and (3) 95% confidence ellipse of the time series.

Dynamic Balance Assessment

An individual is prompted by our GUI to perform the "star reaching task", where we ask the individual to lean as far as possible in the indicated direction. After getting the individual to reach in all 8 directions, the 8 points are plotted and the area inside these points are calculated to give our index to measure dynamic balance. This indicator is called the dynamic balance area.

System Debugging with Able-Bodied Individuals

Seven able-bodied individuals ran through the training session to test the effectiveness of the system. Three out of four games (y-axis) showed bivariate correlation between balance ability (x-axis), indicating the games were appropriately designed.

Future Direction

One problem was there was not a large range in some game scores (the y-axis ranges for the ellipse and colour matching games are lower), making it difficult to distinguish an individual's balance ability with game scores. Immediately after the trial run, this was addressed by changing the scoring system to favour individuals who could keep their balance position closer to the centre of the game targets.

The next step is to conduct another trial run on able-bodied participants, this time incorporating functional electrical stimulation with visual feedback training. Once done and further system optimizations are carried out, the system will be used for clinical trials on individuals with spinal cord injuries to determine the effectiveness of this rehabilitation tool.

Vehicular Rear-View Camera System

Looking forward to looking backwards

Vehicles come in all sorts of shapes and sizes. There are an infinite number of ways you can categorize a car: luxury, sedan, van, truck, vintage. Through countless design iterations over a century, while the rest of the vehicle has adapted to fit modern times, the wing mirror has not seen any development. First, the wing mirror is a major safety hazard. It creates blindspot zones that has made it necessary for drivers to look away from where they are driving to check their blindspots. Second, the wing mirror is the only part of the vehicle that extrudes out of the body of the car. Although relatively small, this obtrusion causes up to 7% of total vehicle drag during driving. Automakers are pushing to eliminate the legislation that requires vehicles to have anachronistic wing mirrors, and this project displays a prototype of what the future of wing mirror-less vehicles might look like.

Compact Rear-View Camera Module

Inner Components

Due to the scope of the project, the system was designed around an off-the-shelf Raspberry Pi camera board, serving as the geometric constraint for our module. To not further increase the size of our system, a custom PCB was fabricated and placed over the camera board. The PCB includes high power IR LEDs for night time viewing, heater connections for de-icing, a temperature sensor, and an ambient light sensor.

Outer Housing

The outer housing was designed to minimize drag created by this system. This camera module would be placed on the outside of the vehicle near the wing mirror. The housing prototype is 3D printed and provides a water-tight seal from the outdoor environment. A dual bandpass filter is placed in front of the camera lens.

Aerodynamic Benefit

Here is a size comparison between a typical vehicular wing mirror and our camera system. After running a simulation for a standard highway driving condition, our camera system reduced the drag by 99.5%.

*I do not take credit for the housing design and optimization or the FEM simulations to verify the drag reduction.

Safety Enhancement: Blindspot Elimination

On the left, a car is passing another vehicle. The large frame is the camera view, which shows the passed vehicle a few seconds before it appears in the smaller frame (side mirror view). A schematic is shown in the top-left figure, showing the elimination of the blindspot of vehicles in the adjacent lane. Once a vehicle in the adjacent lane disappears from the camera view, it will enter the peripheral view.

Safety Enhancement: Low Lighting Conditions

Comparison of our camera system with IR LEDs turned on and off driving through the same highway section with very few streetlights. With the LEDs turned on, the lane markings and much of the road is clearly visible. For comparison to what a side mirror looks like, it would be pitch black. This camera system allows us to see better than human vision, and not reliant on another vehicle's headlights for rear visibility. This also allows us to see potential moose, bears, zebras, kangaroos, or camels that sneak up on us. As far as I know, they don't have headlights.

Alarm Clock Bed

Have you ever slept through your alarm and missed an important meeting or exam? Are you the type of person that hits the snooze button 25 times every morning? Do you typically lie in bed in a depressing state of Weltschmerz, requiring all the will and strength in your mind and body to drag yourself out of bed? We've all been there. Mornings are the worst and traditional alarms clocks just don't cut it. This collage of memes evokes society's frustration with this common problem.

The Blue Clock

Introducing the solution to all your morning problems. A bed that literally kicks you out of bed. The tilting alarm bed acts as a normal bed unless the alarm is triggered. Once activated, a latch mechanism retracts causing one side of the bed to tilt over along with you. The bed will no longer lock in horizontal position for 30 minutes after activation, forcing you to not only wake up, but not climb back into bed. Stay woke.

*I do not take credit for the mechanical design of the bed.

Proof-of-Concept Functional Prototype

Once designed, an initial prototype is built out of Popsicle sticks. This prototype allows us to visualize the vision and assist with elucidating the direction for future design iterations. For instance, pinch points become more obvious and the observed drop height and drop speed help with understanding the mechanics of rolling out of bed. Shown is little Matt Damon rolling out of bed in style.

Putting it Together

The design was shortly finalized and a trip to Home Depot was planned. After some arduous labour, the bed was built. Below are a few components that make up the bed, specifically the mechanisms involved with triggering the alarm.

Full Scale Functional Prototype

Here is the bed in action. Instead of the traditional approach of waking up through sound, this bed uses the sense of (an excessively aggressive) touch. This alarm clock comes with a 100% guarantee of waking someone up. In addition to feeling the impact of the ground, the activation of the latch also creates a gunshot sound. Although promising in its effectiveness, further persistence of getting this product to market would be met with too many lawsuits.
