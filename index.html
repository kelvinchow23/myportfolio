Reconfigurable Display

Can you fit a laptop into your pocket?

Moore's law drives technological advancement to make electronics smaller, true for most cases. Yet, the cellphone, arguably the most pervasive electronic device, defies this trend. Why is this? One reason is the trade-off between convenience and practicality. People would rather carry around a lightweight phone in their pocket. Most people also want large electronic displays, which offer more utility. The electronic display is the geometric constraint of cell phones, tablets, and laptops. A device with a reconfigurable display would maximize the convenience and utility benefit of electronic devices.

The general approach in this research field is to place rigid LEDs on soft flexible, stretchable substrates (see figure below). The key issue with this approach is once the substrate is stretched, the pixel density decreases, reducing the image quality. Also, this approach is dependent on the substrate's mechanical properties, which diminish when stretched. This project proposes a different approach to this problem.

The Initial Design

The first design proposed a solution where 16 individual square "islands", each representing an LED, would be constrained to move uniformly. Each island was constrained to move linearly on a track and connected to other islands via guide arms. By moving one island, all islands would move simultaneously.

Two major problems of this design forced a redesign. First, this design required extra space in order to work, defeating the purpose of having a compact design. Second, this design shared the same flaw as the flexible substrate design. Gaps are created when the islands are in "stretched" state, reducing the pixel density.

Re-Designing the Prototype

The complete assembly of the prototype is shown on the left. From left to right, the figure shows a transition from collapsed to expanded state. Depending on which direction the force is applied, the display prototype can either be stretched from its collapsed 3x3 state to either a 5x5 or a 3x5 shape (shown in a later slide). Different aspect ratios are beneficial for different applications.

Bringing the Design into Reality

(a) After finishing the design, all the components are 3D-printed. (b) After assembly, the prototype has the ability to expand and collapse. (c) LEDs are wired so each level of LEDs can be independently controlled. (d) One LED is placed on every island.

Bringing the Design into Reality

The figure on the left shows the expansion and collapsing of a 2x2 version of the reconfigurable display. The figure in the middle shows the scalability of the display using the current prototype manufacturing method of 3D printing. The prototype's height can be scaled to under a centimetre. The figure on the top right demonstrates the constant pixel density after expansion. The green lights show the pixels on the original top layer and the red lights are from the bottom layers.

This limitation of this design is the large height. The height to pixel area needs to be reduced, or else these displays would be cube-like objects. Nevertheless, this design shows a different, counterintuitive approach to flexible displays.

Balance Rehabilitation Game

Tagline

Individuals suffering from spinal cord injuries (SCI) often have balance instability caused by a reduced neural control of lower-limbed muscles. Rehabilitation tools have been demonstrated to be effective in repairing the disconnect between brain and body. Two rehabilitation tools of interest are visual feedback training (VFT) and functional electrical stimulation (FES) therapy.

Wii Fit is a commercial example of visual feedback training. Some of the training games and used to improve balance and strength training.

MyndMove is a commercial example of functional electrical stimulation. Electrodes are placed on the body to activate certain muscle groups to produce functional movements.

VFT + FES Therapy

We propose a balance rehabilitation tool that combines both visual feedback training with functional electrical stimulation therapy. Our hypothesis is By combining an engaging training game with stimulation therapy, we hypothesize our balance training tool would amplify and accelerate the rehabilitation effects compared to single-modal approaches. For more details, refer here.

My focus on the project was creating the visual feedback training system, which included designing and programming the training system, conducting preliminary trials, and analyzing trial data for system future-proofing. Another lab member worked on the functional electrical stimulation, and the two parts of the project were merged at a later date.

Four Task-Oriented Games

One training session includes 4 games played 3 times each. The games are designed to emulate a collection of muscle strengthening exercises for balance rehabilitation. Each game displays a moving coloured dot on the screen, displaying the individual's centre of position on the force plate.

TARGET: This game trains an individual to stand as still as possible in the optimal balance position. The objective is to keep the red dot as close to the centre as possible.

HUNTING: A randomly spawning blue target appears and the objective is to move the red dot inside that target and hold the position for 3 seconds. Once successful, a new target appears in a different location. This game trains muscle reflexes by inducing constant shifting of body weight.

ELLIPSE: A target follows an elliptical orbit at the edge of an individual's range of motion. While the dot is in the target, the target position increments along the orbit path. This forces the participant to keep their centre of balance away from the middle for the entire duration of the game.

COLOUR MATCHING: In this game, the dot changes colour. The individual needs to move the dot in the matching coloured target. Once successful, the dot changes to a different colour. This game requires some mental processing as well as causing drastic shifts in body weight.

Two Balance Assessments

In addition to the games, the training session included two balance assessments to track progress of balance improvement over multiple training sessions. These assessments were standard tests that have a strong correlation to an individual's balance ability.

Static Balance Assessment

An individual is asked to stand on the force plate with their eyes closed as still as possible for 100 seconds. During this time, a centre of position (COP) time series will record and track the involuntary sway of the individual. For these studies, three performance indicators are taken: root means squared in the (1) anterior-posterior (front to back) and (2) medial-lateral (side to side) direction, and (3) 95% confidence ellipse of the data.

Dynamic Balance Assessment

An individual is prompted by our GUI to perform the "star reaching task" where we ask the individual to lean as far as possible without losing balance in the indicated direction. After the individual reached in all 8 directions, the points are plotted and and joined to create an octagon. The area of the octagon is the performance indicator for dynamic balance, which is called the dynamic balance area.

System Debugging with Able-Bodied Individuals

Seven able-bodied individuals ran through the training session to test the effectiveness of the system. Three out of four games (y-axis) showed bivariate correlation between balance ability (x-axis), indicating the games were appropriately designed.

Future Direction

One problem was the clustering of game scores for some games (the y-axis ranges for the ellipse and colour matching games are lower). This made it difficult to distinguish an individual's balance ability with game scores. Immediately after the trial run, this problem was addressed by changing the scoring system to favour individuals who could keep their balance position closer to the centre of the game targets.

The next step would be to conduct another trial run on able-bodied participants, this time incorporating functional electrical stimulation with visual feedback training. Once done and further system optimizations are performed, the system would be used for clinical trials on individuals with spinal cord injuries to determine the effectiveness of this rehabilitation tool.

Microfluidics

Tagline

Microfluidics is the manipulation of fluids at low volumes in devices with micron-level dimensions. This field of study has become increasingly popular, especially in biological applications. In general, the advantages of microfluidics includes its quick, cheap, and simple manufacturing process, the ability to scale designs for high-throughput studies, and its increased measurement accuracy due to the increased area-to-volume ratio. Related to biology, microfluidic devices are typically bio-compatible, and the small volumes are advantageous since many bio-samples are expensive and often difficult to extract at large quantities.

Bioprinting is the technology intertwining 3D printing and biology. Bio-printers range in capabilities, with some focusing on biocompatible materials that can be used for implantation while others deposit bio-inks containing living cells. Bio-printing is a promising technology with its potential applications in personalized healthcare.

At the Guenther lab at the University of Toronto, one research focus is developing microfluidic bioprinting technologies for tissue engineering. As most of my work is currently in progress and unpublished, it will not be disclosed in this portfolio. I will instead highlight some smaller microfluidic projects and its potential applications in another field. A couple of the Guenther Lab microfluidic bio-printing technologies are shown to the right.

Molecular Gastronomy with Microfluidics

Molecular gastronomy is where food meets science to flip traditional culinary practices. With the same ingredients, the perception of taste can be altered using molecular gastronomy. Here, we focus on using microfluidics to generate microscale edibles. (Please be prepared to exercise some level of imagination.)

3 different manufacturing techniques to create 3 different microfluidic devices to create 3 different dishes will be described. The manufacturing process flow to create the devices are similar. All 3 devices are flow-focusing devices with two solutions: sodium alginate and calcium chloride (CaCl2). The gelation of alginate depends on the ionic reaction between sodium alginate and calcium ions, which creates edible, tastless calcium alginate gels. All devices contain a junction where alginate-filled channel is sandwiched in between two CaCl2-filled channels. By controlling the flowrate ratios, the dimensions of the produced gels can be tuned.

1) Mould Design

The first step is design. A reusable mould is typically involved to make multiple devices, so the design is often of the mould instead of the final device. Here, a 3-layer microfluidic device is shown. The top and bottom layers are hot embossed, while the middle layer contains lasercut through holes to connect between layers. The black holes are through holes, the green patterns are alginate channels, and the light blue patterns are CaCl2 channels. The centre through holes on the top and bottom layer are for the fluidic inlets. Fluid flows from the centre of the device and exits at the 16 outlets at the perimeter.

2) Mould Making

Two moulds are required to create the top and bottom layers. a) A PMMA master is micro-milled. (b) An epoxy-based resin is poured onto the master and fills in the cavities. (c) After setting, the stiff elastomeric epoxy is removed from the master and the epoxy mould is created.

Falooda!

3) Pattern Transfer

The hot embossing process is shown in the schematic, where a mould is pressed against a PMMA slab. The right image shows the resulting embossed PMMA layer.

4) Assembly

After two PMMA layers are embossed and the middle PMMA layer is lasercut, the three layers are bonded together via solvent bonding. Pipette tips are epoxied around the two inlet holes, used to connect to external tubing.

5) Fiber Formation

Alginate and CaCl2 are loaded into 2 syringes mounted on a syringe pump. Tubing is connected to the device, which is placed in a water bath for fiber collection.

Fibers are pulled out of the bath for examination. Fibers of different diameters can be extruded based on the inlet flowrates.

Here is our dessert on some ice cream. The dish that was attempted was an Indian dessert, kulfi falooda. For sanitary reasons, the fibers were extruded differently for consumption.

Bucatini!

Bucatini is a tube-shaped pasta made from an extrusion process. With a PDMS microfluidic device, we can produce candied micro-bucatini (alginate-based tubes with sub-millimeter diameters). Refer here for details on the microfluidic device. Apart from shrinking food to alter taste perception, a dish can be elevated through presentation. Through the controlled deposition of our bucatini extrusion, we can impress with presentation, psychologically enhancing the perception of taste.

Our bucatini maker consists of three syringe pumps (1) that are connected to a microfluidic device (2). The bucatini extrudes from a glass tube (3) and onto a plate fixed to a moving XY stage (4). All components are controlled through the custom GUI (5).

Caviar!

Spherification is a commonly used technique in molecular gastronomy to create solid spherical gels. Using a flow-focusing microfluidic device similar to the falooda maker, oil and alginate emulsions can be formed to make alginate droplets, which can then be later gelled in a bath of calcium chloride.

This device is a five-layer lasercut PMMA device with 5 flow-focusing junctions. All patterns are cut all the way through. Most flow focusing junctions look similar because the physics of droplet breakup is favourable. Instead of altering the junction, the design focused on seamlessly integrating the junction in a more aesthetic design. The flower in the design is a stylized version of the Bauhinia blakeana, featured on the flag and emblem of Hong Kong.

This is the device after bonding. The inlets are at the top of the device and the outlet is at the bottom.

Vehicular Rear-View Camera System

Looking forward to looking backwards

Vehicles come in all sorts of shapes and sizes from luxury cars to common sedans, from hybrids to vintage cars, or from trucks to Smart cars. While the rest of the vehicle has morphed to fit modern times, the wing mirror design has stagnated and looks more or less the same on most vehicles (see vehicles below). Over time, there have been incremental design changes to the side mirror and a drastic overhaul is necessary since the wing mirror is a major design flaw.

First, the wing mirror is a safety hazard. It creates blindspot zones, which has made it standard procedure for drivers to keep their head on a 180° swivel to perform blindspot checks before changing lanes. This flaw has existed for over a century and has never been fully addressed. At fast speeds, fractions of a a second can be the difference between life or death. The safest option is for drivers to always keep their eyes on the road in front of them. Second, the wing mirror is the only part of the vehicle that unexpectedly extrudes out from the body of the car. Although relatively small, this obtrusion can cause up to 7% of total vehicle drag during driving.

Automakers are pushing to eliminate the law requiring vehicles to have these anachronistic wing mirrors. This project previews the future of wing mirror-less vehicles.

Compact Rear-View Camera Module

Inner Components

Due to the scope of the project, the system was designed around an off-the-shelf Raspberry Pi camera board, serving as the geometric constraint for our module. To not further increase the size of our system, a custom PCB was fabricated and placed over the camera board. The PCB includes high power infrared (IR) LEDs for night time viewing, heater connections for de-icing, a temperature sensor, and an ambient light sensor.

Outer Housing

The outer housing was designed to minimize drag created by this system. This camera module would be placed on the outside of the vehicle near the wing mirror. The housing prototype is 3D printed and provides a water-tight seal from the outdoor environment. A dual bandpass filter is placed in front of the camera lens.

Aerodynamic Benefit

Here is a size comparison between a typical vehicular wing mirror and our camera system. After running a simulation for a standard highway driving condition, our camera system reduced the drag by 99.8%.

*I do not take credit for the housing design and optimization or the FEM simulations to verify the drag reduction.

Safety Enhancement: Blindspot Zone Elimination

On the left, the camera (large frame) and side mirror (small frame) views show our car being passed by another car. A schematic is shown on the top-right. At first, the car is in both the camera and side mirror view. When the car leaves the side mirror view, it enters the normal blindspot of our vehicle. Yet, in our camera view, the passing car is still in frame and only leaves the frame when the car has fully overtaken our vehicle. Thus, our camera eliminates the blindspot of cars in the adjacent lane.

Safety Enhancement: Low Lighting Conditions

Here is a comparison of our camera operating at night through the same highway section. The left is our camera with the infrared LED turned on, and the right is the camera with the light off. The right view is similar to the view of the side mirror. It is clear from these videos that the IR LED improves visibility at night. When the car enters the section without streetlights (or any other external light), the IR LED allows the camera to pick up the lane markings on the road. In comparison, the side mirror would have shown a pitch black view, demonstrating how dependent drivers currently are on external sources of light. This camera module allows the driver see potential vehicles with their headlights off (or moose, bears, zebras, kangaroos, or camels) that sneak up on us.

Demo Day

This project was for a fourth-year undergraduate capstone project, where we displayed the potential advantages of a camera-based rear view system to the University of Waterloo engineering community. For demo day, we showcased an interactive experience of our camera system on our supervisor's Porsche, demonstrating the safety benefits of our system. With the monitor placed in front of the driver, we demonstrated a driving experience without having to tilt your head to check blindspots.

Due to the limitations of time and money, the project was narrowed down to a manageable scope, demonstrating a few key concepts. Ideally, we would have put an emphasis on seamlessly integrating the monitors into the vehicle's dashboard to improve the driving experience. Another emphasis that was briefly covered was the integration of safety features using object detection. There are numerous other ways in which technology can be used to improve our driving experience, so why not use it?

Alarm Clock Bed

For those mornings where you need a little push

Have you ever slept through your alarm and missed an important meeting or exam? Are you the type of person that hits the snooze button 25 times every morning? Do you typically lie in bed in a depressing state of Weltschmerz, requiring all the will and strength in your mind and body to drag yourself out of bed? We've all been there. Mornings are the worst and traditional alarms clocks just don't cut it. This collage of memes evokes society's frustration with this common problem.

The Blue Clock

Introducing the solution to all your morning problems. A bed that literally kicks you out of bed. The tilting alarm bed acts as a normal bed unless the alarm is triggered. Once activated, a latch mechanism retracts causing one side of the bed to tilt over along with you. The bed will no longer lock in horizontal position for 30 minutes after activation, forcing you to not only wake up, but not climb back into bed. Stay woke.

*I do not take credit for the mechanical design of the bed.
