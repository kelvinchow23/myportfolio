Reconfigurable Display

Can you fit a laptop into your pocket?

Moore's law drives technological advancement to make anything containing electronics smaller, true for most cases. However, arguably the most important, pervasive electronic device, the cellphone, defies this trend. Why is this? If you were to narrow it down to one reason, it is the trade-off between convenience and practicality. Everyone would rather carry around a lightweight phone in their pocket, but people also want large electronic displays, which offer more utility for just about every application for phones other than using the phone as a phone. This display is the geometric constraint of most cell phones. This trade-off between convenience and practicality is even more visible with tablets, a once considered niche product between a phone and a laptop.

Research in this field place rigid LEDs on soft flexible, stretchable substrates. The key issue with this approach is once the substrate is stretched, the pixel density decreases, reducing the image quality. This project proposes a different approach to this problem.

The Initial Design

The first design proposed a solution where 16 individual square "islands", each representing an LED, would be constrained to move uniformly. Each island was constrained to move linearly on a track and connected to other islands via guide arms. By moving one island, all islands would move simultaneously.

Two major problems of this design forced a redesign. First, this design required extra space in order to work, defeating the purpose of having a compact design. Second, this design shares the same flaw as the flexible substrate design, where there are gaps in between islands, reducing pixel density when stretched.

Balance Rehabilitation Game

Individuals suffering from spinal cord injuries (SCI) often have balance instability caused by a reduced neural control of lower-limbed muscles. Rehabilitation tools have been demonstrated to be effective in repairing the disconnect between brain and body. Two rehabilitation tools of interest are visual feedback training (VFT) and functional electrical stimulation (FES) therapy.

VFT + FES Therapy

We propose a balance rehabilitation tool that combines both visual feedback training with functional electrical stimulation therapy. Our hypothesis is By combining an engaging training game with stimulation therapy, we hypothesize our balance training tool would amplify and accelerate the rehabilitation effects compared to single-modal approaches. For more details, refer here

Four Task-Oriented Games

One training session includes 4 games played 3 times each. The games are designed to emulate a collection of muscle strengthening exercises for balance rehabilitation. Each game displays a transient coloured dot on the screen, displaying the individual's centre of position on the force plate.

Two Balance Assessments

The training session also includes two balance assessments to track progress of balance improvement over multiple training sessions.

ELLIPSE A circular target follows an elliptical orbit along the boundaries of an individual's range of motion. While the dot is in the target, the target position increments along the orbit path. This game forces the participant to keep their centre of balance away from the middle for the entire duration of the game.

Seven able-bodied individuals ran through the training session to test the effectiveness of the system. Three out of four games (y-axis) showed bivariate correlation between balance ability (x-axis), indicating the games were appropriately designed.

Vehicular Rear-View Camera System

Looking forward to looking backwards

Vehicles come in all sorts of shapes and sizes. There are an infinite number of ways you can categorize a car: luxury, sedan, van, truck, vintage. Through countless design iterations over a century, while the rest of the vehicle has adapted to fit modern times, the wing mirror has not seen any development. First, the wing mirror is a major safety hazard. It creates blindspot zones that has made it necessary for drivers to look away from where they are driving to check their blindspots. Second, the wing mirror is the only part of the vehicle that extrudes out of the body of the car. Although relatively small, this obtrusion causes up to 7% of total vehicle drag during driving. Automakers are pushing to eliminate the legislation that requires vehicles to have wing mirrors, and this project shows a prototype of what the future of wing mirror-less vehicles might look like.
